Special Attack




A special attack is an offensive ability typically used on a targeted creature or area of creatures, though occasionally used on an object or the environment.

To build a special attack, you pick Effects below. Unlike with other effects, all of the Special Attack effects bundle together automatically, and you select the Perimeters (the number of attacks required, range, area, targets, etc) for the entire combined special attack all at once, instead of improving those things with boosts.

By default, Special Attacks:

  • Take One Action
  • Are not Strikes, and are their own unique actions

Multiple Attack Penalty:
The second time in a round that a creature makes an attack (of any kind) that will deal direct (not persistent) damage, they are -5 to the roll. Any times beyond the second time, they are -10 to the roll. Other attacks are not subject to the MAP.

Here are a few other rules an modifications you can consider:

Regarding Multiple Defense Types

As you build a special attack, it may require multiple types of defenses, such as Willpower AND Fortitude. You always make a single attack roll, but since creatures have varying values in their defenses, it is possible only some sections of your special attack will work. This is fine, only apply the parts that actually succeeded. Sometimes it makes sense to have one effect be a required hit in order for the others to even stand a chance (for example maybe you shoot a poison needle, and the needle attack must hit in order for the secondary effect which allows a fortitude defense to even apply.) In those cases, you will make multiple rolls.

Terminal Damage

An individual attack may only benefit from the two greatest increases to its regular damage currently affecting it. Each additional source of damage increases its damage only by +1.
This represents a sort of 'critical mass' for an attack, the point where the attack is simply either deadly or not.



Example:

  • You have a special attack called "Power Attack" that takes 2 actions and deals +1d10 damage in addition to your regular strike.
  • You then have a special attack called "Kick Em While They're Down" that 'adds to strike' when an enemy is prone, dealing an additional +2d6 damage.
  • Then a party member casts a spell on you called "Lighting Weapons" that adds +1d8 lightning damage to your strikes.
  • Instead of your longsword (1d8+10 normal damage) attack dealing 1d8+1d10+2d6+1d8+10 damage to the prone foe, you can only benefit from 2 of the three boosts fully.


    • You would probably pick the lowest damage boost you have, which is "Lighting Weapons" Instead of getting 1d8, now it only gets +1 damage.
    • In Total your strike's damage would be 1d8+10 (Base)+1d10 (Power Attack)+2d6 (Kick Em While They're Down) +1 (Lightning Weapons)

Building Afflictions


You can also design an attack to be an Affliction. An affliction always requires either a Fortitude or Willpower attack, even for types of damage or conditions that specify other types of defenses. When designing an Affliction, you'll be purchasing three or four different attacks as a linked ability, each representing a different stage of the affliction. If you want your affliction to deal damage, use the persistent damage prices.

  • Stage 1 Afflictions: Regular Value
  • Stage 2 Afflictions:
    Mod -4
  • Stage 3 Afflictions: Mod -8
  • Stage 4 Afflictions: Mod -12

Onset: The default onset is instantaneous. If the onset is different, reduce the overall cost of the attack:

  • 1 Round: Mod -2
  • 1 Minute: Base -4
  • 1 Hour+:
    Base -12
  • 1 Week+: Base -16

Durations: The duration of each stage is determined by the duration selected for the condition. If no duration is applicable (because you only select damage, for example) then the duration is 1 round. Damage always only occurs once per stage regardless of the duration.
At the end of the duration of a stage, the target gets another defense to see if the affliction progresses or regresses as per normal affliction rules. By default, the maximum duration of an affliction can be no longer than 3 times the final stage.

Remember that any time you deal damage a round, it counts toward your Multiple Attack Penalty. Afflictions are no exception to this.

Multiple Exposures: Each successful exposure of an affliction increases its stages by 1.

Sophie's Choices

If you want your target to have the option to pick their own poison, so to speak, you can pick this feature. Here you can design two different sets of effects. The Base of the combined effects is equal to the average of the two (so add them together and divide the sum total by two - round up.) Use the same formula to calculate the Mod. When you target a creature, it can pick which effect it is subject to.

Sweetening the Deal

You can apply positive effects to otherwise offensive abilities, giving the enemy some benefit as well as harmful effects. If the benefit you give them is actually beneficial almost all of the time, you can reduce the Base of the offensive ability by half the Base of the beneficial one (instead of paying at all for the beneficial one.)
If the beneficial effect only applies if the enemy defends against your attack, then the reduction is only one third the Base of the beneficial one, and if the beneficial effect only applies if the enemy critically defends against your attack, the reduction is one fifth of the cost.

Note that the spirit of this option is to give interesting though impact-lessening effects to your offensive abilities. An example would be a "suppressing fire" ability that penalizes your target's attacks while increasing their guard defense as they dive for cover. GMs should not allow abilities with these options that don't follow that spirit.

Effects

There are many types of Effects, allowing you to have truly unique abilities. Each effect has its own rules and limitations, so make sure to read each carefully. You apply limits and boosts to each effect separately, making sure that the whole ability makes sense and that they don't contradict each other. Remember, the GM has final approval on all abilities.

The effects are assumed to trigger on a successful roll. If the effect requires a critical success, then reduce the base cost of the effect by half, and the Tier by one. If you want the effect to trigger on a failure (not possible to make this a critical failure) then double the base cost and increase the Tier value by one. These modifications are made before limits and boosts are applied.

Many attacks are actually "Linked Abilities" that have varying results based on the attack roll, such as doing half-damage on a failure, full damage on a success, and double damage on a critical success.

Inconsistent Damage:
You may want an attack to deal damage that varies based on factors that may not always be in your control. For example, an attack that shoves an enemy back 30 feet (see the forced movement effect below) may also deal 1d6 damage per 5 feet of movement IF the target hits a barrier. In these cases, work with the GM to determine what the most likely dice outcome you will experience is, then pay for the dice amount between the maximum and that most likely number (round up.)

Disruptive

Largely for reaction attacks, this ability disrupts certain types of actions. Disruption can have any defense type.



Pick your disruption criteria:

  • Critical Hit: Your disruption occurs only if you critically hit.
  • Double Success: If this special attack includes another effect that requires a defense roll, and you select this option, then disruption occurs if you both hit with that other effect and a separate roll to attack with this one.

Force Behavior



Defense Type: Will

You force a creature to perform a series of actions. By default, this causes at least two specific mechanical penalties to a creature: It loses actions and possibly provokes reaction attacks. This ability is also powerful because of its abstract nature, which could have a number of tactical applications depending on the nuance of the actions you are forcing. No forced behavior is powerful enough to cause a creature to commit blatant suicide, though it can force it to do something that greatly increases the risk of death. You can force a creature to walk through a barrier that it is unaware of, for example, but not to walk through a barrier it knows deals lethal damage.





Maximum Number of Possible Actions Lost:

  • 1 Action (Base: 4)
  • 2 Actions (Base: 8)
  • 3 Actions (Base: 12)
  • 6 actions (Base: 16)
  • One Minute (Base: 16, Mod +3)
  • One Hour (Base: 20)




Setback of Actions:

  • No other setback: Action is flavor ("Say your name backwards" - "Jump up and down" - "Stand there and do nothing", etc) (Standard)
  • Minor Setback: Action has the manipulate or concentrate trait, possibly requires a distribution of free hands ("draw an item from your pack", "Cast a non harmful cantrip", "Slap yourself in the face with an open palm.") (Mod +4)
  • Moderate Setback: As minor, but possibly leaves them in an inferior position ("Lay down and roll backwards", "Throw one of your possessions as far as you can," "Hand over one of your possessions to a friend of mine", "Repeat the actions you performed last round.") (Mod +8)
  • Major Setback: As moderate, but possibly is completely detrimental to them or harmful to them or their allies ("Strike your friend with your sword," "Throw something you are holding as far as you can, then run in the opposite direction," "Hand me one of your healing potions," "Give me your most valuable item.") Note this effect is still not powerful enough to force blatant suicide. (Mod +12)

Perimeters

The Perimeters of a special attack are all the other elements beyond the raw effect of the attack. With other effects, you typically use Limits and Boosts to modify these aspects, but with Special Attack, they are cooked in as part of the entire ability itself.

Attack Type

You must select which attack type your attack uses. Some effects specify that they modify the overall special attack, and these often don't require attack rolls at all. In order to have a completed special attack however, you must have some effect that requires an attack type, or be modifying a strike somehow (see next section.) Most effects that require attack rolls specify which type of defense is required, but some give you multiple options. Simply specify which defense is required for each effect in your Special Attack.

Limit: Guard Defense. Because Guard defenses are typically higher, especially if the defender is using a shield or has cover, treat any effect that requires a Guard defense as having a limit with reduction of (Mod -4)

As you build a special attack, it may require multiple types of defenses, such as Willpower AND Fortitude. You always make a single attack roll, but since creatures have varying values in their defenses, it is possible only some sections of your special attack will work. This is fine, only apply the parts that actually succeeded.



The exception to this rule is if it makes sense to have one effect be a required hit in order for the others to even stand a chance (for example maybe you shoot a poison needle, and the needle attack must hit in order for the secondary effect which allows a fortitude defense to even apply.) In those cases, choose the limit: Linked: Secondary Attack Effect for each effect that requires the initial attack to succeed. Then roll those defenses separately and after the initial roll to hit.

Instead of requiring an attack roll, you can instead take the following boost:

Boost: Automatic Success

(Base +8)

No attack rolls are required, this effect immediately takes effect against the target. If you deal damage, do not add the +10 default damage



Attacks that Include Strikes

Special Attacks are typically their own move, not actually "strikes." They typically don't care what kind of weapon you are using, as they have their own range and results. There are three key perimeters you can select that change this:

Attack is Innate Strike

[Base +1, Maximum Base: 7, Mod +6]

This attack is its own weapon or strike. If any ability specifies a "strike" this attack can qualify.
This is often taken to represent a martial arts style, or a natural weapon of some kind. There are two key rules to taking this boost:

  • The majority of the Strike's damage must be bludgeoning, piercing or slashing.


Attack Includes a Strike or Maneuver

This type attack includes and requires a successful strike or combat maneuver (chosen at creation), following all of the rules of a normal martial Strike or maneuver - including range, attack modifiers, etc. The strike itself makes a guard attack. If the strike misses, all other secondary effects - even if they require separate defense types - also miss. If the strike hits, then, as usual, compare the same roll to the other defense types to see if they also hit. You don't add your +10 damage more than once, no matter what effects you pick.

For adding raw direct damage, use the base and mod costs of persistent damage.

Special Note: You can combine attacks with this option with separate attacks that "Add to Strike," but only if they do not have the same (or very similar) Situational limit conditions as Base Limits.

Target Modification: Source Object


(Boost: Base +4)

Your attack actually causes an object to be the source of the attack, such as superheating a piece of metal or causing a worn object to radiate necromantic energy. Any creature ending its turn holding or touching the object is subject to the attack at that time. By default, using this effect on an unattended object requires no roll, but using it on an attended object requires an attack vs Reflex on the wielder. Thereafter, each time they are subject to the attack they get a Fortitude or Willpower defense (whichever is most appropriate).

  • The attack cannot have the "Direct Damage" effect, but it can have the "Persistent Damage" effect. In this case, there is no flat check to remove the Persistent Damage, instead the damage is dealt as part of the overall ability with an attack as described above. The default duration of this damaging effect is 3/1d6 rounds.
  • The overall duration of an ability with this boost is equal to the shortest duration effect in the ability. That is the duration the object continues to be a 'source' of the attack. The duration of any condition given to someone touching it is automatically changed to "until the end of their next turn."

Area Modification: Zone



[Boost: Base +4]
Default Duration of Zone: 2/1d4 rounds (can be improved with boosts)

Your attack is a zone. Your area of effect will deal your attack's effects to any creature there when the zone is created, and to any creature who ends their turn in the zone. All normal defense rolls apply.

The duration of any condition caused by the zone attack automatically become 'until the end of the target's next turn'. Direct damage is done immediately. Afflictions and Persistent Damage have their normal durations and interactions.

  • Enhanced Zone: (Mod +6) Your zone targets anyone who *enters* the zone at all, not just ends their turn there.

Secondary Target Effects

With GM approval, when you build a Special Attack that affects more than one target, you can design a separate effect to targets beyond the first. Picking this modification costs Mod +2, but allows you to design a separate Special Attack power of equal value to the first (without spending any points beyond the Mod +2) If your secondary target effects are weaker than the primary, reduce the cost of the area effect or multiple targets by an equal amount to the difference in cost (to a maximum reduction of 50%). You cannot toggle between the two options at will - your first target MUST be for the first effect, and all additional targets for the secondary effect.
